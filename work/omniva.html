01 / Context

The product had matured.
The experience hadn't caught up.

Platform screenshot — inherited state

Omniva is an AI-native cloud infrastructure console. Engineers use it to manage clusters, storage, and workloads — operations where confusion has a real cost.

The product had been built for technical capability first. The homepage reflected that — it showed what the system could do, not what engineers needed to do next. Datadog showed low engagement and low time-on-page across core sections. Session recordings showed engineers landing and leaving within seconds.

The product was technically ready to grow up. This is what building that foundation looked like.

02 / My Role

First designer.
Only designer.

I joined Omniva at the very beginning — before there was a product. Three years, three phases.

Early on: user research from scratch, personas, end-to-end journey mapping across customer-facing and internal platforms, and the operational platform for the teams onboarding clients. As the product scaled, I owned complex workflow design across the console — designing what was requested, shipping what was needed.

At a certain point the product was technically mature enough for something more systematic. That's when this initiative began. There was no discovery practice, no research rituals, no analytics loops. I introduced all of it — Datadog reporting, session recordings, monthly interviews — under delivery pressure, while shipping.

I worked in a product trio with PM and Engineering Lead. That structure didn't exist before either.

Validation loop

Analytics → wireframe → engineering feasibility → client validation → iteration → ship → repeat. No concept went in front of users without engineering alignment on feasibility first.

03 / Process · Phase 1 — Foundation

Fix the structure before
redesigning the surface.

Before touching any screen, I mapped the full navigation. What I found was a product built feature-by-feature — sections grouped by engineering logic, not by how engineers actually worked. Legal and resource links had ended up in the footer. No comparable infrastructure platform had this pattern. Competitor analysis confirmed it. We moved them to the header under Docs & Legal.

The restructure addressed four things in parallel: information grouping, header cleanup, footer removal, and UX copy.

Why the footer mattered

Legal and resource links — Service Terms, Privacy Policy, SLA, Developer Portal — had ended up in the footer. Right content, wrong location. No comparable infrastructure platform had this pattern. Competitor analysis confirmed it. We moved them to the header under Docs & Legal, where they're accessible without disrupting the operational workflow.

04 / Process · Phase 2 — Homepage

The navigation was fixed. The homepage still didn't know where you were.

Two types of engineers used the same screen. New engineers who had never created a cluster. Operational engineers who ran production workloads daily. The homepage gave both the same thing — raw utilisation charts and subscription data. Neither group could use it.

The surface had no concept of where you were in the journey. It couldn't tell if you'd just added billing or were managing twelve clusters in production. That became the question every iteration tried to answer differently.

Does guidance change behaviour? /

Guided empty states

Hero CTA pattern across every empty state. Engineers had a starting point for the first time. Session depth improved — but recordings revealed a second problem.

Can they see what they own? /

Metric cards + Cluster Overview

Account Overview removed — subscription data replaced with live cluster status. Engineers could see their infrastructure. But seeing and understanding are different things.

What does the system need from me right now? /

Signal-driven hub

Adaptive surface responding to user state. System health first-class. Built on six months of evidence collected while previous iterations were live.

What the data showed

Session depth improved for new engineers. But recordings revealed something unexpected — engineers with existing infrastructure were ignoring the homepage entirely. They'd land, see a CTA they didn't need, and navigate away. One answer, two completely different needs.

Iteration 1B — "Can they see what they own?" · Metric cards + Cluster Overview

The gap 1B couldn't close

Engineers could see their clusters were running. They still couldn't tell if something was wrong. There was no health signal — no way to know if 94% GPU utilisation was expected or critical. Seeing infrastructure and understanding system state are different problems. 1B solved the first. The signal-driven hub solved the second.

One more structural change — Alerts

Testing the new homepage revealed that Alerts — still nested under Monitoring — weren't being found intuitively. Engineers looked for them directly, as a standalone action item. We promoted Alerts to top-level navigation. The usability data made the case clearly.

05 / Collaboration

How decisions actually got made.

Product trio — PM, Engineering Lead, and me. Every major decision was a shared direction, not a design proposal waiting for approval.

PM · Competitor analysis + CTA positioning

Where should the primary action live?

PM brought competitor analysis showing most platforms placing the primary action in the top-right corner. I defended top-left positioning — the strongest visual anchor based on F/Z scanning patterns, supported by multiple UX studies I shared. We reviewed the evidence together and aligned on top-left. The decision was grounded in how engineers actually scan a dense operational interface, not convention.

Engineering · Sequencing the build

The right solution, built in the right order.

The signal-driven hub required significant backend work. We sequenced deliberately — foundational iterations first, each one adding a layer the next could build on. Ongoing discovery ran in parallel throughout, validating that we were moving in the right direction. By the time the hub was ready to build, the foundation was in place and the direction was confirmed.

Engineering · Defining "healthy"

What does system health mean to an engineer?

"Healthy" couldn't be implemented until we agreed on a definition. I worked with Engineering to map out what engineers actually needed to know — threshold values, warning vs. critical states, and which signals were reliable enough to surface. The collaborative definition process shaped the final signal hierarchy on the hub.
